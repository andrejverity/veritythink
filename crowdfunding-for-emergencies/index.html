Crowdfunding for Emergencies

Crowdfunding for Emergencies - OCHA Think Brief

Written with Javier Salazar and Roxanne Moore; August 2105

Read the full report

AI generated summary:

Traditional humanitarian funding systems often face significant gaps between required resources and actual contributions, with unmet needs reaching record levels in recent years. To address these shortfalls, the practice of distributed small-scale fundraising via digital platforms is being explored as a vital supplement to established systems. By leveraging technology to collect many small donations from a large group of people, this approach can deliver funds directly and immediately to disaster-affected areas, complementing slower, traditional humanitarian aid cycles.

For maximum effectiveness in emergency response, a hybrid model combining donation-based and reward-based structures is recommended. While traditional debt or equity models are less suited for immediate crisis relief, a hybrid approach utilizes community recognition and digital "badges" to maintain donor engagement without the high logistical costs of physical rewards. Implementing a "distributed-goals" framework further enhances transparency by allowing donors to see how different funding levels directly impact specific projects, such as rebuilding schools or providing medical supplies.

Building and maintaining trust remains the most critical factor for successful emergency fundraising. Humanitarian actors must navigate risks at individual, community, and system levels to ensure "do no harm" principles are upheld. Partnering with established global agencies can provide a layer of verification, acting as a "trusted agent" that validates local NGO projects. This verification, combined with automatic reporting to financial tracking services, increases accountability and ensures that private contributions are used effectively to support affected populations.

Ultimately, the future of emergency funding lies in the integration of innovative digital tools, including mobile money and peer-to-peer payment systems. While no single existing platform currently meets all the criteria for ideal humanitarian use, strategic partnerships between international organizations and crowdfunding platforms could revolutionize how the world responds to sudden-onset disasters. By moving from days or months to minutes in the release of international funds, these tools have the potential to save more lives through immediate, community-led action.
