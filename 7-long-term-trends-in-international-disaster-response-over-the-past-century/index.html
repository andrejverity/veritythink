7 Long-Term Trends in International Disaster Response Over the Past Century

Written with RJ Reid; November 2018

AI generated summary:

This report evaluates the long-term evolution of international disaster response by comparing the humanitarian efforts following the 1907 Jamaica earthquake and the 2010 Haiti earthquake. By analyzing shifts across seven key areas—alerts, mobilization, communication, delivery, finance, non-governmental organizations, and international cooperation—the study identifies a century-long trend of drastically increasing speed and capacity in crisis response.

While the twentieth century was defined by technological and process-driven advancements that prioritized response velocity and volume, the analysis suggests a shift in the coming decades toward prioritizing the quality of aid. As existing systems mature, emerging technologies like artificial intelligence, drones, and blockchain are expected to move the focus from centralized service delivery toward decentralized, community-led empowerment.

Ultimately, the report forecasts that future disaster response will be characterized by a shift in the roles of international actors, moving from primary service providers to facilitators and coordinators. By leveraging new tools to improve information sharing and direct financial support to affected populations, the humanitarian system aims to overcome challenges such as infrastructure damage and complex, climate-driven emergencies while fostering greater local engagement and autonomy.
