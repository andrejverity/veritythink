Guidance for Collaborating with Formal Humanitarian Organizations

Guidance for Collaborating with Formal Humanitarian Organizations

Written with Annie P. Waldman and Shadrock Roberts; September 2013

Read the full report

AI generated summary:

This guide provides a foundational framework for Volunteer and Technical Communities (V&TCs) and technology companies seeking to establish effective partnerships with formal humanitarian organizations. It bridges the gap between traditional humanitarian response systems and the evolving landscape of digital humanitarianism by offering practical advice on navigating organizational structures, mandates, and operational cultures. By understanding these differences, non-traditional actors can better formulate requests for partnership and maximize the benefits of collaborative efforts.

The report details the distinct characteristics of formal humanitarian entities, including United Nations agencies, non-governmental organizations (NGOs), and government humanitarian agencies. It highlights the importance of the Humanitarian Principles—humanity, neutrality, impartiality, and independence—which govern how these organizations maintain access to vulnerable populations. Furthermore, it explains the formalized "Cluster Approach" used to coordinate large-scale relief efforts, providing tech groups with a clear roadmap of where their digital contributions can most effectively fit within established sectors.

Navigating the deployment cycle is a central focus, covering four critical stages: pre-activation, activation, deployment, and post-deployment. Each phase presents unique opportunities for collaboration, such as pre-disaster relationship building to establish trust and post-deployment evaluations to measure impact and accuracy. The guidance emphasizes that strong communication, clearly defined focal points, and a nuanced understanding of institutional "red tape" are essential for overcoming common obstacles like data privacy concerns and varying views on new technology.

By aligning the specialized technical skills of digital volunteers with the extensive experience and global footprint of formal organizations, partnerships can significantly amplify the impact of humanitarian aid. This collaborative model aims to reduce redundancy in response efforts and ensure that innovative digital tools—ranging from crowdsourced mapping to statistical research—directly meet the urgent needs of affected populations.
